## Abstract

Systematic review of 25 studies examining sleep's role in exercise recovery. Sleep restriction (<6 hours/night) for even one night reduced maximal strength by 5-8% and power output by 7-12%. Chronic sleep restriction (<7 hours/night for 5+ days) led to cumulative performance decrements of 10-15%. Sleep deprivation reduced testosterone-to-cortisol ratio, increased perceived exertion at matched workloads, and impaired glycogen resynthesis. Injury risk increased by 1.7× in athletes averaging <8 hours of sleep compared to those averaging ≥8 hours. Sleep extension (increasing sleep duration to 9-10 hours/night) improved sprint times (2-4%), shooting accuracy (9%), and reaction time (5-10%) in multiple studies.
